# House Style Profile — "On Web 3.0 Data Space" (Post-Platforms Foundation)

*Derived from 5 LinkedIn newsletter articles, Aug–Oct 2024. Author: Alex Tourski (4 of 5); Egor Yakovlev (1). Source: the "On Web 3.0 Data Space" newsletter, Post-Platforms Foundation.*

---

## 0. Scope warning (read first)

This is a **single-genre, single-dominant-author** corpus. All five pieces are the same genre — a long-form LinkedIn evangelism essay — and four are by one author. That means the methodology's central split (constant *house voice* vs. variable *genre convention*) **cannot be performed here**: the two collapse into one fingerprint. What follows is best understood as *"Alex Tourski's W3DS long-read voice,"* not a multi-genre house style. To get a true house/genre separation you would need other genres from the same organisation (web copy, a deck, emails, a whitepaper).

The corpus also **uses US spelling, heavy em-dashes, and frequent hyperbole/overclaiming** — three things that directly contradict standards in your Meaningfy work. If the goal is to reproduce *this* voice on its own terms, that is fine and the profile below is faithful to it. If the goal is to fold it into Meaningfy's house voice, it conflicts head-on. (Flagged again at the end.)

## 1. House Voice (the fingerprint)

**Register & formality.** Semi-formal, conversational-authoritative. Educated but deliberately plain — never academic. Contractions throughout ("we've", "isn't", "you're", "don't").

**Tone & attitude.** Evangelical and missionary. Contrarian against consensus and incumbents; optimistic-to-utopian about the future. Diagnostic framing — problems are a "disease" with a "cure", a "silver bullet", an "octopus", an "elephant in the room". High certainty, low epistemic caution.

**Stance toward reader.** Teacher walking a student forward. Constant invitations: "Read on.", "Dive in to find out.", "Let's start by...", "Prepare for a journey, much like in *The Lord of the Rings*."

**Person & address.** All three persons, sliding fluidly:
- **"we"** — the Foundation / humanity / author-and-reader together (dominant);
- **"I"** — personal anecdote ("I spent the first 25 years of my life in the USSR", the author's 70-year-old mother and her 200 passwords);
- **"you"** — direct, frequent, often in hypothetical scenarios ("Imagine the ATM recognizing you...").

**Sentence habits.** Active voice dominant, low nominalisation. Medium sentences (≈15–25 words) punctuated by very short declaratives, often as standalone one-line paragraphs for emphasis: "It was a good start.", "Data ownership.", "I see this as a huge problem.", "Of course not."

**Lexical character.** Coined, capitalised proprietary terms with "e-"/"W3" prefixes (eVault, eReputation, ePassport, Persistent ID, Identity Pyramid, Cerberus services, Community Economics, prosumers). Real tech/policy jargon used fluently but always explained (PKI, X.509, SSL/TLS, eIDAS, GDPR, KYC, linked data, "triples", Solid PODs). Brand names as concrete illustration (Uber, Facebook, SAP, Booking.com, BMW, YouTube, Siemens, Amadeus). Emphatic intensifiers: "exactly", "absolutely", "truly", "simply", "merely", "indeed", "of course".

**Conspicuous absences.** No footnotes or formal citations (links are inline: "see this article"). Almost no hedging. No data tables. No crude language. Body text is mostly prose, not bullets.

**Punctuation & formatting fingerprints.**
- **Em-dashes** as a habitual rhythm device — everywhere.
- **Ellipses** for suspense ("…security by design!").
- **"(!)"** to flag the surprising ("without any built-in security measures (!)").
- **"***"** dividers between major movements.
- **Bolded lede** paragraph(s) opening each article (the abstract/hook).
- **Pull-quote emphasis paragraphs** — the article's thesis beats set larger/indented (e.g. the "single silver bullet" statement).
- **Scare quotes** around coined or contested terms ("closed ecosystems", "flat markets", "prosumers").

## 2. The signature rhetorical engine

This is what actually makes the writing recognisable. Reproduce these or the imitation fails:

1. **One controlling metaphor per article.** Each piece is built on a single vivid conceit and returns to it throughout: the *elephant* (Blind Men parable), the *octopus* (Standard Oil), the *kettle / house colour* (brain vs database), the *Identity Pyramid* + *Lord of the Rings* ("One Key to Rule All"), *doors for the keys* (security).
2. **Rhetorical questions as pivots.** Sections open or turn on a question, often self-answered: "Is this even possible? Absolutely!", "What went wrong? Is eIDAS a poor standard? No...".
3. **Concrete everyday + brand-name examples** for every abstract point (a hotel paying 20% to Booking.com; BMW's 1,000 silos; a Whirlpool washing machine ad following you for two months).
4. **History/cross-domain analogy** to carry the argument: Standard Oil's break-up, the American frontier, the USSR, email vs messengers, airline (1%) vs hotel (20%) commissions.
5. **Hypothetical second-person scenarios** ("you're traveling by boat, it sinks...").
6. **Authority epigraphs / name-drops** — Confucius ("Let's agree on names..."), Tim Berners-Lee, the Buddhist Tittha Sutta.
7. **Sweeping economic claims with big round numbers** — "€1 trillion annually", "€0.5 trillion per year", "boost GDP by several percentage points". (This is the overclaiming tell.)
8. **Chatty parentheticals and asides** ("if you recall NFT at this moment, it failed", "remember managing Google Doc access for your team last week?").

## 3. Genre structure (the W3DS long-read)

Purpose: persuade a professional LinkedIn audience that a tangle of separate problems is one problem (data ownership), and that W3DS is the cure. Length ≈1,500–3,500 words.

Typical skeleton:
1. **Banner** with the headline typeset *into the image* (see §5).
2. **Bolded lede** — frames the problem, promises a payoff, ends on a hook ("Read on", "Dive in to find out").
3. **Parable / anecdote opener** (optional but common): the elephant, the mother with 200 passwords, the USSR childhood.
4. **Problem diagnosis** — enumerated pains, heavy on examples.
5. **Mechanism / solution** — the W3DS feature set, each coined term introduced and explained.
6. **Implications / future vision** — "The Future Economy with...", utopian payoff.
7. **"***" dividers** separating movements.
8. **Visionary close** — "Welcome to Web 3.0 Data Space!", "For the first time, data will truly serve society."
9. **Byline** — "Author: Alex Tourski, Founder and CEO of Post-Platforms Foundation".
10. **"Explore the world of Web 3.0 Data Space in our library:"** — numbered cross-links to sibling articles.
11. **Hashtag block** (#w3ds #dataspace #digitalsovereignty …).
12. Occasionally **"About Post-Platforms Foundation"** boilerplate + CTA ("Build with us if you are…", website, info@postplatforms.org).

Section headers are descriptive and often question-form or evocative: "The Name of the Game", "Is There Even an Elephant?", "The Terra Incognita".

## 5. Visual / banner analysis (the part you asked about)

Every article carries a custom banner; one ("Data Elephant") embeds a true explanatory diagram. The pattern across the five:

**a. The headline lives inside the image.** Title is typeset large as part of the banner, not just above it.

**b. The image literalises the article's controlling metaphor.**
- *Fixing Digital Economy* — people walking into a luminous swirling vortex/tunnel; gold-orange gradient + photo montage (the journey into a new economy).
- *Data Elephant* — a hand-drawn black-and-white **cartoon** of blind men touching an elephant labelled "DATA SPACE", each figure and thought-bubble tagged with a sub-problem (Fake News, Vendor Lock-in, Data Silos, IPR Control, Participative Democracy, Reputation) and an initiative (MyData, blockchain, SOLID, Gaia-X, IDS, European Digital ID). This one *argues the thesis visually* — many partial views of one whole.
- *Brain Outpaces Databases* — a glowing blue neural-network brain on a dark "digital" field.
- *Security Service Booster* — dark bank-vault doors ringed by a constellation of app/service icons; yellow headline.
- *One Key* — a large golden key radiating outward, encircled by stylised service icons (envelopes, locks, carts) on a purple circuit-board field.

**c. Two visual registers.** Most banners are glossy, high-saturation, AI-generated-looking "techno-sublime" (glowing networks, keys, vaults, vortices). The exception is the deliberately **old-fashioned hand-drawn cartoon** used when the piece hinges on a parable/framework (the elephant). Rule of thumb: *vision piece → glowing futuristic imagery; parable/framework piece → labelled hand-drawn cartoon.*

**d. Consistent brand furniture.** A small "Web 3.0 Data Space Library" wordmark in a corner and the teal "PP" foundation logo on the masthead.

**Instruction for reproducing the visuals:** generate a banner that (1) sets the headline as styled text inside the frame, (2) literalises the article's one central metaphor, (3) picks the glowing-futuristic register for forward-looking pieces and a labelled hand-drawn cartoon when the article is built on a parable or framework, (4) carries the library wordmark + foundation logo.

## 6. Confidence & gaps

- **Well-evidenced:** the rhetorical engine (§2), proprietary lexicon, US spelling, em-dash/ellipsis habits, article skeleton, banner pattern. These recur across all five.
- **Medium confidence:** the "pull-quote emphasis paragraph" device — inferred from the rendered layout; verify against the live LinkedIn pages.
- **Cannot be determined from this corpus:** how the voice flexes across *other* genres; whether traits are organisation-wide or author-specific (4/5 are one author). Egor Yakovlev's single piece ("Brain Outpaces Databases") is slightly more measured and uses more first-person-singular reasoning, hinting at mild author variation — too thin to generalise.
- **To confirm with you:** target spelling (US as-is vs convert to UK), and whether overclaiming/big-number rhetoric is a feature to keep or a defect to strip.
