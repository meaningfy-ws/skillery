# W3DS Library — House Style Profile & Generation System (v0.2)

Corpus: 8 LinkedIn newsletter articles from *On Web 3.0 Data Space* /
Post-Platforms Foundation. Batch 1: *Wish List Fulfillment*, *eReputation*, *Why
Businesses Need W3DS*, *From Fear to Control*, *What's Wrong With Blockchain?*
Batch 2: *Sovereign Identifiers*, *Interoperability of platforms via eVaults*,
*Future of Work and Adult Learning*. Authors: Alex Tourski (CEO), one by Egor Yakovlev.

> **Scope flags.**
> 1. **One publication, several macro-structures.** Every piece shares a single
>    house voice; they differ by structure. Five sub-types are now distinguished
>    (Section 4). The voice layer is genuinely constant; the structure layer varies.
> 2. **Not the Meaningfy voice.** US spelling, heavy spaced em-dash use, confident
>    unhedged assertion — the inverse of Meaningfy's stated standard (UK spelling,
>    em dashes banned, no-overclaiming). Keep this a separate profile unless you
>    rule otherwise.
> 3. **Quality is uneven.** Typos, occasional broken grammar, and inconsistent
>    number formatting appear throughout. These are defects, not style; a
>    reproduction must not imitate them.

## 1. House Voice (constant across all 8)

- **Register:** Semi-formal, essayistic, accessible to an intelligent layperson;
  educated but deliberately non-academic. Conversational asides throughout.
  Evidence: "Spoiler: Not really."; "Can you picture it? Good."
- **Tone & attitude:** Evangelistic, confident, movement-building; optimistic about
  W3DS, polemical toward rivals (Web 2.0 platforms, blockchain). Value-laden framing:
  "digital lords"; "the nightmare of corporate data management".
- **Stance to reader:** Collegial-persuasive and didactic; the reader is a prospective
  convert walked through an argument and invited to join. "We invite you to explore";
  "How about you? Join the project."
- **Person & address:** "We" = the foundation/movement; first-person "I" for grievance
  or field anecdotes (the EC portal saga, the Frankfurt symposium, the hospital visit);
  pervasive "you/your". Imperative openings: "Imagine…", "Consider…", "Let's…".
- **Sentence habits:** Mixed length; medium explanatory sentences (~15–28 words) broken
  by very short punch lines. Active voice dominant. Punch beats: "It's your problem.";
  "Both scenarios are unacceptable."; "You choose."
- **Lexical character:** Very high proprietary-coinage density; capitalised concept
  nouns; analogy and metaphor as the primary explanatory engine; liberal cultural and
  historical name-dropping (Orwell's *1984*, *Her*, Harari, Berners-Lee, Satoshi,
  the Luddites, the French Declaration of Rights, Confucius, Churchill).
- **Conspicuous habits/absences:** Usually no citations even for hard statistics
  (occasionally cited for a headline figure, e.g. Gallup/Korn Ferry on the $8T loss);
  little academic hedging; minimal corporate throat-clearing.
- **Punctuation/formatting fingerprints:** spaced em/en dashes; rhetorical-question or
  provocation section headers; a **bold lead/hook paragraph**; standalone bold or
  blockquoted "pull-quote" sentences as emphasis beats; ellipses for dramatic trailing;
  question-then-answer rhythm; an epigraph occasionally opens the piece.

## 3. Rhetorical Device Library (the distinctive layer)

Deploy 2–4 per piece, not all at once. This is what makes the writing recognisable.

1. **Two-futures bifurcation** — present a fork between dystopia and flourishing.
2. **Promise-vs-reality debunk** — list a rival's promises, knock each down ("Promise N: X — Actually, No").
3. **Imagined dialogue** — a short invented scene. *Use unnamed/composite actors, never real named people.*
4. **Named-persona walkthrough** — narrate through Alice/Bob to make an abstraction concrete.
5. **Everyday vignette** — small relatable scenario (stepladder, retired gardener, seven hospital systems).
6. **Sustained analogy** — one carried metaphor per major point (grain & feudal lords; car keys for private keys; 2D snapshot vs 3D object; learning a foreign language for interoperability).
7. **Historical-sweep opener / long-horizon projection** — "Since the dawn of humanity…"; "look 15 years into the future".
8. **Epigraph opener** — a short quotation to set the frame (e.g. the Confucius lines).
9. **Numbered enumeration** — requirements, options, components, or use cases.
10. **Options-triad forcing function** — collapse a choice to three (government / platforms / yourself).
11. **Pre-empting objections** — an NB/disclaimer aside up front, or a "Concern:" / "Addressing Concerns" Q&A block.
12. **First-person grievance anecdote** — a lived frustration as evidence (the EC portal saga).
13. **Multi-stakeholder POV sweep** — walk the same idea through user → company → policymaker → universities.
14. **Library cross-linking + hashtag close** — numbered sibling-article list and a hashtag block to end.

## 4. Genre Macro-Structures (five sub-types, one voice)

- **Vision essay** (*Wish List*, *eReputation*): hook scene → status-quo limitation →
  "imagine instead" → mechanism → second-order social benefit → invitation. ~1,200–2,000 words.
- **Polemic / debunk** (*Blockchain*, *From Fear to Control*): hook → stakes → systematic
  teardown (promise-vs-reality or two-futures scenario) → "here is the alternative" →
  reflection / key takeaway. ~2,500–6,000 words, sub-headed chapters.
- **Requirements brief** (*Why Businesses Need W3DS*): context → scope ("what we mean by…")
  → numbered requirements, each with a stakeholder line and a bold "requirement" line →
  rollout outlook → conclusion. ~3,000 words.
- **Explainer / systemic deep-dive** (*Sovereign Identifiers*, *Interoperability*):
  optional epigraph → problem statement → proposed mechanism → enumerated
  properties/components → "Concern:/Addressing Concerns" Q&A → "how real is it /
  from vision to execution" status close. 2,000–5,000 words, numbered sections.
- **Use-case cascade** (*Future of Work*): bold italic hook → current-situation diagnosis
  → "unleash imagination" → numbered named-persona use cases → synthesis (the unifying
  concept/diagram) → multi-stakeholder discussion → rollout → invitation. ~4,000–6,000 words.

## 5. Visual house style (reproducible)

- **Cover banner (every article):** a coloured brand panel carrying the bold title, set
  beside an AI-generated illustration that literalises the article's core metaphor,
  plus a small "Web 3.0 Data Space Library" tag. Mood: optimistic, editorial-stock.
- **Diagram family A — clean corporate vector:** rounded boxes, arrows for relations,
  eVault cylinder icons, a "Real World" element in the centre, layered pyramids, corner-node
  triangles. Colour code: **blue = platforms / Web 2.0 / current**, **green = post-platforms /
  Web 3.0 / desired**. Used for systemic and architectural points.
- **Diagram family B — hand-drawn marker sketch:** loose black line art (e.g. the "Plans
  for Next Week" slider board). Used for the human, whimsical, or personal-agency points.
- Guardrail for generated imagery: no copyrighted characters, real people, brand logos,
  or film/TV artwork (the original *Her* poster insertion is a rights risk; do not imitate).

```
HOUSE VOICE — W3DS Library essay
Write as the Post-Platforms Foundation: an advocacy voice building a movement
around Web 3.0 Data Space.

DO:
- Address the reader as "you"; speak as "we" for the foundation.
- Open with a hook: a vivid scene, a long-horizon framing, or an epigraph.
- Carry every abstraction with a concrete analogy, vignette, or a named persona (Alice).
- Vary rhythm: medium explanatory sentences broken by short punch lines.
  e.g. "It's your problem."
- Use the canonical lexicon exactly (W3DS, eVault, eReputation, eName, Sovereign Identifier…).
- Pre-empt the obvious objection with an NB aside or a "Concern:" Q&A.
```

### 7A. Vision essay
```

INPUTS: {{capability}} · {{status_quo_limitation}} · {{mechanism}} · {{social_benefit}}
STRUCTURE: hook scene → status-quo limitation → "imagine instead" → mechanism →

DEVICES (2–3): historical-sweep or vivid-scene opener; everyday vignette; one sustained analogy.
LENGTH: 1,200–2,000 words.
SELF-CHECK: Opens on a scene not a definition? Every abstraction carried by analogy/vignette/persona?
Ends on an invitation? Any stat flagged for sourcing?

```

## 9. How to operate and validate

- **Sample canonically:** 3–5 recent, canonical documents per sub-type; skip outliers.
- **Validate with a holdout:** keep one real article of a sub-type out of the analysis,
  generate a new one from the same brief, and see whether a reader can reliably tell which
  is which. That blind test is the only honest measure.
- **Store and version the profile** (this file) as the durable asset; the prompts are
  derived from it. Re-run when the voice drifts or a new sub-type appears.
