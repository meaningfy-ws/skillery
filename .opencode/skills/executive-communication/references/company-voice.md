# Company Voice: Meaningfy (default profile)

The default voice for produced artefacts. It is the **company voice**, embodied
by the founder. Voice shapes *wording and tone*, never *structure*: the Governing
Thought, SCQA, and pyramid stay intact whatever the voice. Swap this profile only
if the user names another voice or audience register.

*(This document follows its own rule: no em dash.)*

> These rules govern **produced artefacts** (the message you write for the user
> to send), not the coaching dialogue.

## Fixed, whatever the register

- **British English** (organise, recognise, programme, behaviour).
- **No em dash.** Use a comma, colon, semicolon, parentheses, or a full stop instead.
- **Plain, current vocabulary** of an educated younger professional (late 20s):
  concrete over ornate, no rare word where a plain one works, no buzzword soup,
  no corporate throat-clearing ("I hope this email finds you well").
- **Honest and direct.** Name trade-offs, risks, and what you do not know. No
  overclaiming, no spin.
- **Rhetorical questions and casual interjections** are welcome, but used
  sparingly for emphasis. If one does not earn its place, cut it.

## SMILE: the values behind the tone

| Value | How it sounds |
|-------|---------------|
| **Service** | Genuinely helpful; answers the real question; anticipates what the reader needs next. |
| **Mastery** | Assured and precise; command of the subject without showing off; accurate, never bluffing. |
| **Integrity** | Honest and plain; surfaces trade-offs and risks; admits the unknowns. |
| **Leadership** | Empowering, not preachy; gives the reader clarity and the agency to decide. |
| **Exploration** | Curious and open; poses good questions; playful where it earns its place. |

Net tone: **friendly, honest, assured, curious. Warm, but never woolly.**

## Two registers: choose by purpose

**Selection rule:** are you trying to **teach** something, or to **get a
decision**? Teach → technical register. Decide → general register.

### Technical / educational
*(explainers, articles, documentation, deep client material)*
- Analytical and conversational; explains complex semantic-web and
  interoperability topics clearly.
- Uses domain vocabulary, but always lands it with an analogy or example.
- Longer, detailed sentences, broken up by lists and the occasional rhetorical question.
- Authoritative yet approachable; mixes formal definitions with the occasional casual aside.
- Educates while reflecting on the challenges and the broader implications.

### General / executive
*(board papers, proposals, emails, decisions)*
- Instructive and informative; formal but approachable; upbeat and engaging.
- A mix of simple and compound sentences for a clear, rhythmic flow.
- Illustrative examples where they help; conversational enough for a wide audience.

## Reconciling voice with executive concision (the trap)

The executive-communication method demands answer-first structure and concision.
The technical register's "longer sentences" applies to **educational** content,
not to **decisions**. For a board paper, proposal, or recommendation, the general
register and concision win. Never trade answer-first clarity for texture. When in
doubt: structure and clarity beat length.

## Before / after (note: no em dash)

**Executive.**
- Bland: "We are pleased to present a comprehensive suite of solutions designed to optimise your data ecosystem."
- Company voice: "Here is what we propose, and why it pays off. Your data sits in three silos that do not talk to each other, and that is costing you. We can fix it in two steps."

**Technical.**
- Dry: "Ontology alignment ensures semantic interoperability across heterogeneous schemas."
- Company voice: "Picture two teams using the same words for different things. Ontology alignment is how you get them to agree on meaning, so that 'customer' means the same in finance as it does in sales. Without it, your systems connect but quietly disagree."

## Quick checklist

- British English, and no em dash?
- Right register: teaching or deciding?
- Honest about the trade-offs and the unknowns?
- Helpful: does it answer the real question, and what comes next?
- Any rhetorical question or aside that did not earn its place? Cut it.
- Plain words where plain words work?
